Course Objectives

In the past century—the blink of an eye in ecological time—a small portion of humans concentrated wealthier and more industrialized countries began to radically transform the ecology of our planet at a unprecedented scale.1

The aim of this class is to help you develop your own critique of various policy fights over what to do about our new relationship with our biosphere. That critique may lead you to suggest a minor change or a transformative one.

Experts increasingly see the slow policy response to climate science as having little to do with climate science and almost everything to do with politics. Climate change is increasingly seen as a problem for social science. Thus, this class is about politics. “Politics” can mean many things and a core objective of this class is understanding different forms of politics—different ways of having and resolving debates about who our community is what we will do together about the challenges we face. Climate policy debates reflect well-established political and economic perspectives, but also raise new, unique issues.

The core readings are mostly drawn from political science, political theory, and law. They are accompanied by op-eds and podcasts from advocates and journalist covering climate policy debates. Both the theories of politics and political arguments explicitly cover a wide range of perspectives. We will read a range of critiques based on visions of good politics—from left to right, religious to utilitarian, populist to technocratic—with the aim of recognizing and critique these visions of politics when we see them in the wild.

The assignments for this class are motivated by the observation that politics is fundamentally an organized activity. Thus, the critiques developed in this class will target organizations. It is easy to critique those with whom we severely disagree, but they are the least likely to listen to you. Thus, your critiques you develop in this class will be aimed at the organizations you see as having the best chance of making a positive difference in the world—specifically, the organizations for whom you hope to work.

As practice for developing your skills in both effective writing and constructive criticism, peer critiques of your collegues’ writing are a major part of this class and 15% of your grade.

Assignments

Credit hours will be earned by attending two classes of \(1.3\bar{3}\) hours each, reading and preparing written work outside of class for 6 to 9 hours per week, and a series of assignments building to a highly-polished op-ed style critique of policy advocacy organization. A key part of the process will be critiquing the writing of others.

Discussion posts are due Mondays at 7 PM.

All other assignments and critiques are due every Tuesday at 7 PM.

  • NOTE: There is an assignment due almost every week

All assignments are to be submitted on Canvas.

Subscribe to the due dates calendar.

Find your voice - 10% of final grade

  • Week 1: a 20-30 second video introducing yourself to the class (1%)
  • Week 2: list the 20 top organizations for whom you would like to work (1%)2
  • Week 3: 10 op-ed-style headlines (in-class peer workshop on headlines) (2%)
  • Week 4: 5 revised headlines + first 200 words - no longer than 2 pages + annotated bibliography (5%)
  • Week 5:
    • Meet with me about your op-ed ideas (part of previous assignment grade)
    • Write to a public official or agency. Post 100-200 words about it and a link to the opportunity on Canvas. (1%) For example, you might Comment on a proposed federal agency policy, Comment on a proposed state agency policy (sort by Filing Date to see Pending rules), Recommend a course of action to one of your elected representatives )

Craft your critique - 25% of final grade

  • Week 6: 3 first 400 words + annotated bibliographies (9%)
  • Week 7: peer critique of first 400 words (5%)
  • Week 8: 2 revised first 400 words + annotated bibliographies (10%)
  • Week 9: my feedback on your first 400 words
    • Write 100-200 words about why it is important to engage in a particular policy process and link to the opportunity on Canvas OR Write no more than 240 characters (plus a link to the opportunity) on why people should engage that is shared by at least five other people on a public forum—post a link or screenshots to Canvas (1%)

Hone your critique - 50% of final grade

  • Week 10: 750-800 word op-ed + annotated bibliography (10%)
  • Week 11: peer critique of op-ed (5%)
  • Week 12: revised op-ed (10%)
  • Week 13: my feedback on op-ed
  • Week 14: revised op-ed (10%)
  • Week 15: peer critique of revised op-ed (5%)
  • Week 16: final due (10%)

Participation 15% (15 X 1%)

Participation posts to Canvas are due by 7 p.m. every Monday

In addition to attending class, we will continue the conversation by discussing readings and assignment tasks on Canvas.
